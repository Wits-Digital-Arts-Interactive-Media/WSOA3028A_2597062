The Impact of Digital Colonialism on South African Software Developers

Previous Essay
>

The scramble for Africa marked the colonization of most of the continent by seven Western powers. Today, a similar dynamic persists, but it is technologically driven. This essay defines digital colonialism and contextualizes the research object which is the large-scale Google layoffs that occurred in May 2024. By employing Sareeta Amrute’s framework, this analysis will explore whether these technological practices can be considered colonial. The essay will then connect this analysis to the state of South African developers. It argues that while the increase in foreign outsourcing provides jobs to South African developers, it may also be exploitative.

What is digital colonialism?

To understand digital colonialism, we must first define colonialism. According to Amrute, "At its simplest, colonialism is a practice of acquiring full or partial control over another country, territory, or people, occupying it, sometimes with settlers, sometimes indirectly, and exploiting it economically." Colonialism exploits the occupied country’s resources to benefit the colonizer.

Digital colonialism is defined by Danialle Coleman as “decentralized extraction and control of data from citizens without explicit consent through communication networks developed and owned by Western tech companies.” (Coleman 2019).

Research object

In May 2024 Google retrenched 200+ employees including members of its core team. According to Google's website, the core team are responsible for “building the technical foundation behind the company’s flagship products and for protecting users’ online safety.’’. Core teams include key technical units from information technology, its Python developer team, technical infrastructure, security foundation, app platforms, core developers, and various engineering roles.” (Elias, 2024). They are planning to replace this local labor with foreign labor specifically from India and Mexico. These countries offer skilled labor at a fraction of the current cost.

Digital colonization framework

Sareeta Amrute is an associate anthropology professor. Her keynote talk on tech and colonialism will be described specifically her framework for considering whether tech is colonial. This essay will debate the coloniality of Google’s labor exchange as depicted by her.

  1. Is it hierarchical?
  2. Is it extractive?
  3. Is it exploitative?
  4. Does it produce uneven consequences?

1. Technology is hierarchical.

Hierarchies are ranking systems where people at the top of the hierarchy have the most authority, influence and benefits, and these factors decrease as you go down the hierarchy.

2. Extraction

Extractive systems take resources (including labor) from one area to benefit another, often without fair compensation or regard for long-term consequences.

3. Exploitation

Exploitation occurs through labor relations that take advantage of imbalances in power and economic conditions in the world.

4. Uneven consequences

Ms. Amrut explains that “Technological innovation’s collateral damage means that the consequences of development are felt unevenly, with harms accruing to black and brown communities in the United States, and Indigenous, poor, and oppressed communities in Latin America, South Asia, and Africa”.

South African Context

South Africa (SA) is described as an IT hub in the SADEC region. Maureen Tanner in her article “Communication and Culture in Global Software Development: The Case of Mauritius and South Africa," explains how the country’s diverse cultural heritage fosters communication and adaptability, which is an advantage in navigating digital colonization complexities.

Conclusion:

Using Amrute’s framework to analyse the Google layoffs, this essay concludes that these actions can be seen as a manifestation of digital colonialism, fitting the four factors identified by her. By connecting these factors to the South African software development industry, the analysis reveals a nuanced landscape. While the increase in remote work opportunities provides jobs, the significant pay disparities highlight potential exploitation.

Reading Reflection

I chose the reading "Algorithmic Colonization of Africa" by Abeba Birhane. In this article, Birhane compares traditional colonization to digital colonization. He explains that both forms share a common goal: “Common to both traditional and algorithmic colonialism is the desire to dominate, monitor, and influence social, political, and cultural discourse through the control of core communication and infrastructure mediums.” He further discusses how both forms of colonization invent justifications for their actions. For example, a Facebook project justified capturing census data to create a population density map. Facebook essentially assumed the authority to map, control, and create population knowledge, thereby controlling perceived knowledge.

This form of colonialism is subtle yet pervasive, often masquerading as technological innovation and solutions to social problems. Understanding how digital colonialism operates is crucial, though I joked about applying it to my website—unless, of course, I'm serious.

Birhane emphasizes the importance of context-aware solutions and warns against the one-size-fits-all approach that ignores the unique challenges and values of African societies. This is particularly important because Africa is in a completely different context from the Western world. As a developing continent, we face different problems compared to developed countries. We need valid solutions to our problems, not digital colonialism with ulterior motives.

Birhane also discusses ethical considerations that can help decolonize digital practices, primarily the involvement of local communities in the development process. The web and browsers have been inherently Western constructs. However, like music or art, Africa has innovations that haven't been considered yet—a completely new way of doing things and different intentions in our work. For instance, art collected from Africa during colonization was not seen as art by Africans but as tools for rituals. Similarly, what we create might be entirely different from what is currently assumed.

Application to Work

If I were to apply these insights to my website, I wouldn’t consider my work colonialist because I am African—lmao. However, I could research what African websites do differently. I don’t think I need to worry about this issue unless I am making an innovation. Currently, I don’t have the coding skills or knowledge to innovate, so don't hold your breath for any groundbreaking changes.

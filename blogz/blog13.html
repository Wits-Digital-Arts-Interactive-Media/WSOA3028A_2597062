The internet as a moral space

Shani Orgad's Reflection on Roger Silverstone's Views on the Internet as a Moral Space

Shani Orgad's reading sheds light on Roger Silverstone's views about the internet as a moral space. Orgad brings out key points from Silverstone's work that challenge the usual ideas about online communication and interactions.

Interconnected Media Environment

Firstly, Silverstone's view is that the internet isn't separate from other media; it's a part of the broader media environment. He argued that the media environment is interconnected and deeply woven into our daily lives. This means we need to study online activities alongside offline ones since the two are intertwined.

Proper Distance in Communication

Secondly, Orgad delves into Silverstone's concept of "proper distance" in communication. Silverstone believed in a media ethics that recognizes the "other" in communication, stressing "unconditional responsibility" over mere reciprocity. This idea challenges much of the existing discussion about the internet, which often focuses on reciprocity, connections, exchange, and interactivity without considering the moral implications of online communications.

Critique of Connectivity and Sociability

Orgad also highlights Silverstone's critique of connectivity, sociability, networking, and interactivity. He argued that these concepts often overlook the moral aspects of online communication. Silverstone pointed out that closeness and even intimacy online don't necessarily lead to recognition or responsibility, and a focus on reciprocity might exclude those who can't engage in such exchanges.

Anonymity, Disembodiment, and Online Intimacy

Additionally, Orgad touches on Silverstone's concerns about anonymity, disembodiment, and online intimacy in computer-mediated communication (CMC). Silverstone warned that the ability to "switch off" in online interactions threatens our sense of responsibility and weakens the moral duty of listeners to provide unconditional care.
